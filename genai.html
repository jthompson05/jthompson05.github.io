My GenAI Assistant

This project explores the development and epistemological implications of a custom GenAI assistant powered by the Gemini API. To investigate technical communication and user interaction, I built two distinct versions of the assistant: a command-line interface for terminal-based dialogue and a web-based application using the Flask framework. Beyond the code, this documentation reflects on the nature, origin, and limits of machine-generated knowledge based on specific class readings and collaborative discussions.

Reflection

My understanding of the nature of knowledge in Generative AI was deeply influenced by our in-class discussion regarding whether a computer can be sexist, racist, or bigoted. This conversation made me realize that what we often perceive as "knowledge" in an AI assistant is actually a reflection of the "priors" and background context embedded in its training data. As we discussed in class, a computer does not possess an independent moral compass; rather, its "knowledge" is derived entirely from the patterns and biases present in the information it has consumed.

This concept of "social evidence" was further clarified by Alvin Goldman’s A Guide to Social Epistemology. Goldman describes social evidence as information concerning acts of communication by others, such as pages of print or messages on computer screens. This directly applies to GenAI, as the model’s entire origin of knowledge is a massive collection of social evidence—human-generated text. Reading about the distinction between "reductionist" and "antireductionist" views of testimony helped me see the limits of GenAI. If we view the model's output as a form of testimony, we must grapple with the fact that the model lacks the "competence and sincerity" we usually look for in a human testifier.

Despite these insights, I still have questions about the future of GenAI and epistemology. Specifically, if GenAI begins to generate a majority of the "social evidence" available on the internet, will the origin of human knowledge become a closed loop that reinforces its own hallucinations? Furthermore, if an AI can pass a technical evaluation of science despite having hallucinated references, does our definition of "valid science" depend on the process of discovery or the accuracy of the final output?

Referecnes:

Goldman, A. I. (2011). A guide to social epistemology. In A. Goldman & D. Whitcomb (Eds.), Social epistemology: Essential readings (pp. 1-5). Oxford University Press.

Command Line Based Interface

When constructing the command-line GenAI assistant, I prioritized a clean and functional interface that facilitates a seamless back-and-forth dialogue within the terminal environment. My primary design choice was to implement a persistent chat history loop, which allows the Gemini model to reference previous parts of the conversation for improved contextual accuracy. Additionally, I focused on user-centric control by including a specific termination command that triggers a clear "Goodbye!" message, ensuring the program exits gracefully.

Web-Based Interface

When constructing the web-based GenAI assistant, I focused on creating a user-friendly interface using the Flask framework to make the model accessible beyond the terminal. My central design choice was to build a dedicated home page that provides clear instructions, ensuring users understand how to interact with the assistant before they begin. I opted for a web form to handle user prompts, allowing the application to capture input, communicate with the Gemini client, and then dynamically render the AI-generated responses directly onto the webpage for a professional, interactive experience.
